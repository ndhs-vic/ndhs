NEWSLETTER APRIL 2009

 

ANNUAL MEETING

The courthouse was full for the annual meeting, not in a mad scramble for executive positions but in order to hear the guest speaker, Alex Culvenor. Alex’s speech was as good as expected; the committee positions remained unchanged. Apart from some discussion as to meeting times, the main focus of the evening was Alex’s reminiscences of the Newstead parts of his life or as he put it “the growing up and slowing down periods”.

Culvenor is a name that has long been connected to the Sandon/Campbelltown area and one feature of the night was the showing of the farm diaries of Richard Culvenor, farmer of “Honeyvale”, Campbelltown - Alex’s grandfather. There were 35 such diaries and 10 remain in Alex’s possession. In his fortunate life he had a good education, 3½ years in the services, a marriage to Joan Ashburn, in 1945 back to school for a further 2 years, an engineering position in the Defence Equipment Factories, a 2 year posting to London, 10 years later a 3 year posting to Washington DC. Retiring from the Government Aircraft Factory in 1980 they returned to the family farm, “Osnaburgh” in Sandon with a chance to enjoy the hobby of building and flying aeroplanes.

Alex mentioned several individuals who were part of his growing up. Uncle Bill Ashley – a great uncle by marriage – was a retired carpenter who came to live when his parents moved to Sandon in 1921. We were shown some of his home made carpentry tools that were off limits in a chest during his lifetime but which also contained some pliers that gave credence to his reputation as a amateur dentist. Alex’s father would relate stories of Uncle Bill’s courting days when he would walk from Werona to Smeaton. His devotion was successful and he was eventually invited to stay for dinner!

Alex’s father planned and built up the dairy farm at “Osnaburgh” and 1929 saw the first introduction of automation in the milking shed, a steam driven Greensdale milking machine and an Alfa Laval separator. This lasted until the 1940s when the last boiler blew up in a spectacular fashion scattering showers of steam, water, coals and ashes down the middle of the milking shed and onto the 24 cows. From then a combustion engine and a belt driven separator was used. Steam was missed because of its convenience in heating water for washing the machines. As Alex said,  “I believe it was this machine environment that convinced me to study engineering even when I was only part way through primary school”. As well there was a large Ronald & Tippett 12 HP oil engine for cutting chaff and crushing grain. Alex and Claude as 12 and 11 year-olds were allowed to fire up the boiler and start the oil engine but they were never allowed to work the chaff cutter as Alex’s father nearly lost his life when his hand was caught in it. 

Two workers on the farm were Ted Clarke and Ephram Banks or “Banka” as he was known. “Banka” was responsible for the blasting of the solid sandstone to sink the well and passed on to the boys that knowledge and boxing and riding skills. “Osnaburgh” was also the site of the Sandon Telegraph Exchange and Alex’s mother operated it for 40 years.

Other residents of Sandon for special mention were Myrtle and Stan Ebery. They decided to teach the young people of Sandon the skills of dancing – Myrtle on the piano, Stan on the button accordion and Paddy Casey as MC. Stan was a well-known meteorologist and weather forecaster. The Bureau of Meteorology who provided him with instruments for taking measurements knew him well. Much of Stan Ebery’s weather forecasting was relative to the jet stream and he had taught himself trigonometry and maths to make his calculations of the height and speed of clouds from an old 1798 Encyclopaedia Britannica.

As is obvious from this précis of Alex’s talk, it was wide ranging, informative and amusing. A good indicator of a successful night is when the audience linger to chat and by this measure it was a great event.

CHHA MEETING

The quarterly meeting of the Central Highlands Historical Society will be held in the Newstead courthouse at 10 am on Saturday May 2nd. Ina Bertrand will hold a workshop on Oral History. Afterwards the attendees are to lunch at the Railway Hotel.
