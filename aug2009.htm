EXCURSION

A pleasant afternoon was spent as guests of the Carisbrook Historical Society last week. They are good neighbours and are always most friendly and welcoming. A visit had been made several years ago but it was decided that they would have different things to show us after a gap of fifteen years and our society now included members who had joined in the interim.

Carisbrook members were able to show us their present headquarters at the former Town Hall and nearby Maternal Health Centre. There were many different projects going on, including the scanning of an impressive number of photographs. Carisbrook publishes an impressive weekly local newspaper from the same building. They have also been the beneficiaries of a very large book collection that needed to be sorted and sold. (Some of the Newsteadites helped the cause!). Although historical societies have the same sort of goals, it always interesting to see the different approaches that are taken to reach them.

 

FILING CABINETS

When our new Scan Pro was bought, a general rearrangement of furniture occurred. The two 4-drawer filing cabinets ended up in the main room and detracted from the atmosphere of a nineteenth century courthouse. An idea was floated that we could get 2-drawer cabinets that would then fit under the magistrate’s bench. It was decided to do this and four filing cabinets have been bought. At the same time it is an opportunity to read each file as we transfer it and to make notes to be included in the computer database.

Having some space this month is an opportunity to include a couple of interesting articles form the Echo  - July 5th 1916

The first is an incident that happened to a child who went on to become one of our members and who bequeathed us her wonderful collection of books, some of which we retain as the Adelaide Mackie Collection and the remainder of which were sold at a book auction.

 

 

SERIOUS ACCIDENT

On Saturday last, 1st inst., Mr and Mrs Fred Mackie’s only daughter, Adelaide, sustained serious injuries. Mrs Mackie and the child were residing at the residence of Mr C.Smith, Yapeen, for some time past, Mr Mackie having been an inmate of the Castlemaine Hospital for the past 17 weeks. The little girl was playing in the yard, and a neighbour, hearing a scream hurried to the spot, and was horrified to find the child lying unconscious on the ground, with a horse which had been in the yard, standing beside her; she was taken to the Hospital as quickly as possible, where she now lies in a dangerous condition. On examination the child was found to be suffering from concussion of the brain, and bruises.

 

ARBOR DAY

On Saturday last a working bee assembled in Wyndham Street, between Lyons street and Maldon Road for a tree planting ceremony on Saturday next. Early in the day Messrs Tankard and Hutchinson measured the ground and marked out the situation for the trees. During the forenoon the boys picked out a hole each, opposite the properties in which they had an interest, and with the assistance of their “cobbers” from the main street dug 5 holes. Representatives of the following families were at work:- Nokes, Williams, Biddlestone, Kilpatrick, Parks, Jenkins, Slee, Ellis (2) and Blair.

In the afternoon the seniors assembled, and 16, out of the 22 marked out, were dug, and a dray load of soil in each, replaced the earth removed. Messrs H Pryor, and S Clarke did the carting, J Sheehan and P Hanley the loading, while Messrs E A Rowe, Dr Kilpatrick, McMenna, J Nokes, S T Ellis, G Jenkins, G Hawkey, V Wilson, F W Ellis, T F Hutchinson, E A House, and R Tankard, were on the scene.

About 4 o’clock, Miss Jenkins sprang a welcome surprise on the workers by providing scones and tea, which were much appreciated, and which, even those hardened individuals who feared the tea would interfere with work, joined in.

It was decided to plant the whole block with Oriental Planes, and the planting will take place on Saturday afternoon next, 8th inst. Workers are requested to join in, as the carting and fixing of the tree guards, and the planting of the trees, will take some time. Afternoon tea will be supplied by the ladies.

 

Some thoughts on this article – This was no doubt an initiative of the newly formed Newstead progress Association. The names reflect the families living in Wyndham Street at the time and other members of the community. Dr Kilpatrick was the local doctor and lived at Fintona, the house on the corner of Wyndham and Lyons Street opposite the police Station. S T Ellis was the local policeman of whom there is a photo in the Verey collection and a recent article in the Police Gazette. F W Ellis was the newly arrived Head Teacher who went on to teach in Newstead for 33 years. V Wilson was the editor and owner of the Echo. J Sheehan was from the flourmill family and G Jenkins was connected with Colquhoun’s store, now the supermarket. J Hawkey was part of Hawkey and Skate, blacksmiths on the site of the present day ‘Dig’ Café. H Pryor was another blacksmith, at this time situated near the Railway hotel and E A Rowe and S Clarke were councillors.

Did they plant the Oriental Planes? Today there may be 2 planes left but there are certainly some old elms that may date back to 1916 planting.
