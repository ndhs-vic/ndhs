NEWSLETTER AUGUST 2008

 

THANKS

My gratitude goes to Cherie Lawton who took on the task of handling the July newsletter in my absence. Thanks Cherie!

MAJOR DONATION

The Society has been delighted to be the repository for the research that one of our life members, Margaret Di Fiore, has painstakingly put together over many years but is not now able to store. At our recent August meeting, members were shown the collection and expressed their amazement at the details recorded by Margaret and wondered at the hours of work that went into it. The Society is honoured to be the future conservators of this work.

ANNUAL PARTY

The date set is Saturday October 11th at 6.30 pm and the venue will be the courthouse in Canrobert Street, Newstead. An invitation is extended to all our members, particularly our out-of-towners, to join us if they can, for a night of good food and good company. This annual get-together was initiated several years ago when we realised we are so fortunate to have the use of the courthouse. As well as being a venue for our work and serious endeavours, it could also be used to meet on a social occasion and so we decided to have a night to congratulate ourselves on our year’s work.

MARYBOROUGH FAMILY HISTORY DAY

The theme this year is “Do You Know Where You came From?” and will be held at the Maryborough Resource Centre at the corner of Nolan and Alma Streets, Maryborough on Sunday 21st September between 10 and 4. Representatives from nearby Historical Societies and Family History Groups attend and it is possible to discover details of your family history if they were had connections to this area.

CENTRAL HIGHLANDS HISTORICAL ASSOCIATION

This is an early reminder that the annual CHHA Family History Expo will be at Aquinas Campus, 1200 Mair St, Ballarat on Saturday 4th and Sunday 5th of October and the theme for 2008 is “That’s Entertainment!”.

EXCURSION

On 1st September at 1.30 we shall be carpooling from the courthouse at 1.30 pm to explore the early history of hotels in the district. It was suggested that this might be the basis for an excursion on an earlier visit to the local churches had proved to be popular.

The history of the hotels is a large and indistinct theme. There are innumerable mentions of hotels and grog shops and deciding what is fact and what is unsubstantiated will be an ongoing project. However from the various sources it will be possible to give a list of names of the early ones and their probable sites.

However in 1913, a Licensing Reduction Board sat to consider the ratio of hotels to population and The Echo reported it fully with the results published a short time later. So it the knowledge that we have gained from this era that we will concentrate on for this excursion. The hotels in Newstead at that time were the Crown, Newstead, Railway and Shamrock while out of town there were the Farmers Arms at Sandon, Loddon Leads at the junction on the Maryborough Road and at Welshmans Reef and Sandy Creek there were Captain’s Hotel and yet another Shamrock Hotel.

Some were happy to give up their licences but others employed counsel to argue on their behalf for the right to retain them. The local policeman gave evidence as to their reputations and it is possible to get a glimpse of life in Newstead in that era.

In 1920 it appears that six o’clock closing was being advocated and this article is from the Castlemaine Mail on February 12 1920.

“ The introduction of the new regulations relative to the six o’clock closing of hotels and the many restrictions that have been added which the thirsty have to carefully consider, causes considerable anxiety as to the procuring of lemonade at even the stipulated “temperance bar”, subsequent to the closing and battering down of the vicious beverages that have been available for years from 6 am to 11.30 pm. This transformation of the old methods of hotel management would be an eye-opener to the late Tom Marsden who built the Newstead Hotel in the fifties when the old firm of Cobb and Co’s coaches ran 4-horse teams to Ballarat and Castlemaine. There was no limit to the hours of trading then and access to the “all sorts bar” could be made practically at any hour. The late George Brittingham, one of the family of Brittingham that opened up the Thornhill mine a Green Gully then known as Brittingham’s Reef, which yielded 10 oz per ton for a lengthy period and afterwards sold to an English company with all the machinery for a sum that provided the Brittingham family with a competence for life, purchased the hotel. In later years he retired, the late Mr Richard Marks, principal partner in the Maldon Brewery, becoming the proprietor. Mr Marks carried the cordial industry in conjunction with the hotel business and supplied the town and district for many years with every success until he retired when the late Mr Lamb of Chewton became the next licensee and relinquished the business after a few years The late Mr D McLean then became the licensee and later purchased the whole concern from Mr Marks for the sum of £1,000. Mr McLean carried on the business for many years with every success and after his demise the hotel was sold to the present owner Mr P H Harte. This hotel, although having had many occupants, has always retained a high standard for the convenience of travellers and the general public. This is the oldest established hotel in the town and was the meeting place in the early days for the holding of convivial socials and other gatherings. When the Agricultural Shows were a prominent annual fixture and carried on for many years with great success, the hotel was profusely decorated to do honor to a dinner that was annually arranged by the show committee. Two prominent residents, the late Mr H Webb and Mr Church were well-known figures at these functions and were instrumental in adding to the importance of the event by bringing along a brass band from Guildford that caused the town to get greatly excited. These happy times have long since disappeared and now that the new licensing regulations have been introduced and the new temperance bar instituted those who have recollections of bye-gone days will reflect and consider what is to be the ultimate ending of all these changes. It is current talk that another constable is to be added to the local police station. Is it possible that the temperance bar is responsible for the addition?”
