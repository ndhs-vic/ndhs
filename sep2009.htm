CHHA FAMILY HISTORY EXPO

As usual the first weekend in October is the date for the Central Highlands Historical Society Family History Expo but this year there is to be a change of venue. Instead of Aquinas College it will be held at the Ballarat Specialist School in Gillies Street on Saturday 3rd October and Sunday 4th October 10.00 – 4.00 both days. Entry is $5, children under 12 free. The theme this year is “What’s in a Name?” We have decided to mount our display around the street names of Newstead and their link to the Crimean War. It is interesting to think that a conflict on the other side of the world is commemorated every day when we use the names of Canrobert, Codrington and Hilliers etc. The connection is in the timing, the Crimean War occurring at the time when Newstead streets were being named.

 

With that in mind, perhaps a few thoughts about the Crimean War might be appropriate.

(Condensed from Modern World Encyclopaedia Vol 3)

The Crimean War, between Russia on one hand, and Turkey and Great Britain, France and Sardinia on the other, was occasioned by a conflict over the supposed right of the Tsar to protect all Christians in the Sultan’s dominions. In 1853, a Russian Army invaded the principalities of Moldavia and Walachia, and Turkey declared war. England followed her traditional policy of trying to prevent an expansion of Russia that might threaten British interests in India. France, under Napoleon 111, joined forces to increase her prestige and to divert attention from problems at home.

The allies prepared to invade the Crimea. The attack on the naval base, Sebastopol, was the main feature of the war. This campaign is famous for the allied victories at Inkerman and Balaclava, and the charge of the Light Brigade. More important features were the incompetent Allied administration, which led, in spite of the efforts of Florence Nightingale, to the death by cholera and starvation of large numbers of allied soldiers. After a lengthy siege Sebastopol fell in 1855 and shortly afterwards peace was concluded on the basis of allied war claims.

When our original investigation into the street names took place some years ago, there was not the magic of being able to ‘Google’ for information and some new facts have emerged. Hillier Street, which evaded our attempts then, has turned out to be in fact d’Hilliers and referred to Achille Baraguey d’Hilliers. He joined the French army and in the Russian campaign 1813 as aide-de-camp to Marshall Marmont, he lost his hand. In 1854, he was given command of the expeditionary force and captured Bomarsund. He was promoted to Marshal of France and made a Senator. (Wikipedia)

 

The name Peel was thought to remember Sir Robert Peel, well known as Prime Minister of the United Kingdom. He helped create the modern police force (which is where the terms ‘bobby’ in England and ‘peeler’ in Ireland, came from). However his son Captain William Peel was a Captain in the Royal Navy during the Crimean War so it is more likely that it was he who gave his name to the street. At the Siege of Sebastopol, he picked up a live shell with the fuse still burning from among several powder cases and threw it over a parapet. The shell burst as it left his hand. For this he was awarded the Victoria Cross. After the Crimean War, he served in the Indian Mutiny and was knighted. (Wikipedia)

 

Another fascinating story is that of our nearby township of Strangways.

There was superb composure of the white-haired General Strangways, chief artillery officer and veteran of Leipzig and Waterloo, who while riding with Raglan in the rear of Home Ridge, was struck by a canon ball, which shattered his leg beneath him. Turning to one of his staff, he said in a tone of a man asking for a match, “Would someone have the kindness to lift me off my horse?” When carried to the surgeon’s tent and told he was too old to stand the shock of an operation, his only remark was, “Then let me die among my gunners” – a prayer that was answered 2 hours later. (From Battles of the Crimea War by W Barring Pemberton)

 

Tivey Street seems to be named after another soldier but this time an Australian one and not connected to the Crimean War. This raises more questions – was Tivey Street named at a later date? The information on Tivey is that he was an Inglewood boy, an accountant and was in the Inglewood detachment of the Victorian Rangers. In 1900 he embarked for South Africa and was awarded the Distinguished Service Medal for his efforts in the Boer War. After the war he became a member of the Melbourne Stock Exchange but continued in the Victorian Militia. At the start of World War 1, he was in charge of the officers’ school at Broadmeadows. He was appointed commander of the AIF 8th Infantry Brigade. In Egypt his men were dubbed “chocolate soldiers” because, to relieve Moascar, they travelled by train while supporting soldiers marched two days through heat and sand. They were then known as “Tivey’s Chocs”. After his service in France he again returned to the Citizen Military Forces and resumed his career at the stock exchange.
