OCTOBER 2009 NEWSLETTER

 

 

MARKET

Enclosed with this newsletter is a book of tickets for our Christmas Hamper. This raffle is held in conjunction with our November Market and is our major fundraising effort for the year. We are always surprised and grateful at the generosity of our members, especially those who are not able to be present and help on the day.

For those closer to Newstead, help on the day is most appreciated and donations of produce, cakes and biscuits, jams etc as well as items for the famous “white elephant” stall are welcomed. The Christmas Hamper will need to be filled. Offers of Christmas cake and pudding are promised but other items can be dropped in at the courthouse or delivered to Giselle Neall. Then all we need is good weather for Saturday November 21st at Rotunda Park!

 

CHHA FAMILY HISTORY EXPO

Good reports were received of the recent Family History Expo in Ballarat and thanks to those members who represented us there on the two days. The new venue was felt to be an improvement. Our display was not a winner but it seems the standard of displays was very high.

 

EXCURSION REPORT

The visit to Harcourt was excellent. We were treated to an informative and amusing talk from George Milford at the ANA Hall and then invited to view the great efforts being done in archiving their history onto computer and to inspect the museum. The items in the museum are particularly pertinent to the Harcourt area with its apple-growing, granite quarries as well as all the other items that could occur in many small towns over the years. The display is particularly well set out and labelled. To our surprise, we then sat down to afternoon tea - a little different to the usual ‘stand-up cuppa’ beside our vehicles! A carpool to various sites around the district followed. One highlight was a granite railway bridge where we were impressed by the quality of the workmanship of the stonemasons of the day. To alert us to other facts as we drove between stops, we were provided with a commentary in the form of sheets of information to be read in each car.

Doug Jenkin, one of our Melbourne members, joined us as the Jenkin family had links to both Newstead and the granite quarries of Harcourt and the day was made better by seeing the pleasure Doug got from the visit.

Below are some notes taken from an article from the Mount Alexander Mail July 23rd 1907

and supplied by Doug Jenkin

 

THE HARCOURT GRANITE QUARRIES

The Harcourt quarry granite industry may be said to date back from the construction of the Melbourne to Bendigo railway line in 1861-2. Granite was required in large quantities for the construction of bridges, viaducts, platforms and buildings, and this was supplied by several co-operative parties, chief of which was under the supervision of the late Mr. J. Blight, whose father and brothers owned quarries in England, and he had been brought up in the business. After spending some time in the search for a fortune in gold, Mr. Blight turned his attention to the possibilities of trading in granite. The stone quarried for the railway was obtained from surface blocks, and as the colour and quality were not matters of great importance, there was no difficulty in obtaining the supply. After the completion of the railway, monumental yards were opened and it was found that the surface stone was not hard enough to take a good polish and further, it contained iron spots, which though not visible on the freshly cut stone, developed rust spots and streaks on exposure to the weather. A large sum of money was spent in seeking and finding a bed and the results fulfilled all expectations. Advice from visitors from all over the world say that it is the finest grey granite quarry in the world and no other granite cuts so neatly. Proof of its cutting qualities is that it has been found cheaper to ship Harcourt granite to Queensland and South Australia, as the costs of freight are less than the masons’ costs when using local granite.

Among the many large blocks sent from the quarry the largest was the huge rustic monument placed in the Melbourne Cemetery in memory of Burke and Wills. The block was quarried in 1863 and weighed about 32 tons. At the time the country between the quarry and the railway station was unfenced bush with no made road, and being the middle of winter the work of carting this immense block was no mean undertaking. Bullocks and horses were tried in turns but the ground was so soft that the wheels frequently went down to the axles and crab-winches and screw jacks were constantly in use. Days were spent in getting out of a bog. It took 6 weeks to get to the Harcourt station. A specially prepared railway truck was provided and it was taken to Melbourne on a Sunday the rate of travelling being limited to eight miles an hour.

Stones of over 20 tons were provided for piers of Princes Bridge Melbourne and also to the Ballarat Soldiers’ Memorial. The basement and ramps for the Bendigo Sacred Heart Cathedral were from Harcourt granite as are many polished columns that adorn the financial institutions in Collins Street Melbourne. It went to Sydney to the Cockatoo Island Graving Dock and to Brisbane for the Anglican Cathedral. The Burns memorial in St Kilda Road is built entirely of Harcourt granite and it is almost universally used for the bases and pedestals of bronze and marble statues. Although most of the quarry business is for large stone, Blight quarries entered into a contract to supply Bendigo electric tram track with over half a million cubes which were cut from waste pieces from the quarry floor.

 

 

DOROTHY TURNER

The Society has been saddened to hear of the death of Dorothy Turner.

Dorothy, with her husband Bill, were members of the Society in its early days and were instrumental in obtaining the use of the courthouse as our venue.
