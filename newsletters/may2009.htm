NEWSLETTER MAY 2009

 

 

               SUBSCRIPTIONS

Thank to all those members who have already paid and a reminder to those who have forgotten.

 

               CHHA MEETING

The Central Highlands Historical Association held its quarterly meeting at Newstead on Saturday 2nd May. It was good to be able to welcome people from other societies to our venue. The courthouse was full – we used every piece of seating in the place! After the meeting finished, Ina Bertrand of Trentham conducted a workshop on Oral History. This was rated highly by our attendee and Oral History person, Cherie Lawton. A break was taken at midday for lunch at the Railway Hotel.

 

               SHORT STORY TATTOO

The courthouse was again in use on Saturday 16th May for the Short Story Tattoo arranged for the weekend by Neil Boyak. The section at the courthouse was Crime so the choice of the courthouse as a base was apt.

 

          RICHARD & PHILLIPA MARKS.

Does the name Richard Marks mean anything to you? It is a name that has cropped up under a couple of different guises in recent weeks. Some of you may know it from the impressive memorial in the Newstead cemetery – befitting the man and his wife, Phillipa, who lived in Newstead in the era approximately between 1870 and 1900.

 

The first time the name came to our attention was because of the news that the group who propose to establish a community garden were considering land near the Church of England and one of our members remembered that the local paper at the time, had articles about the success of the gardens that Richard Marks had in this general area. While the files and data were being looked at, a local family came in to do some research and Phillipa Marks, Richard’s wife, was connected to their family tree.

 

After sailing from England, Richard first tried his luck in Chile before arriving in Australia during the gold rush. With his party he was tunnelling into a hillside at Yapeen which was then known as Five Mile Flat. Prospects were not good and he sold up his house and furniture. He had sent for his wife to join him and was considering a move back to Chile, but reports of that place were so doleful that he was convinced to stay in Australia. The party cleaned up the dirt taken from the tunnel which turned out better than expected. The mining venture was enough to start him on his entrepreneurial ventures, which included the Yapeen Hotel, the Castlemaine Criterion Hotel, and the Kangaroo Hotel at Maldon – the latter being most successful. “The Maldon field was at its prime and travellers were arriving on foot and in every conceivable conveyance at all hours of the day and night. Mr Marks found it expedient to obtain a licence to open all night as well as all day”. He then bought into the Maldon Brewery and a hotel at Lauriston before leasing the Newstead Hotel. He bought up land on both sides of the Loddon, cleared it of hundreds of trees that had been washed down and to the ridicule of some, preceded to enhance the soil with hundreds of loads of sand and manure. On the west side of the Loddon, he had lucerne and raised cattle and on the east side he had built his villa. “St Austell” with a fine

garden and hundreds of fruit trees. He went in for fruit growing and cattle fattening and at the latter he was most successful. Two of his beasts were slaughtered in Maldon and “each was found to contain close on a hundred-weight of kidney fat and 150 pounds of caul fat”. He had the first windmill in the district erected, a fishpond and fountain and reports of his 2½ pound pear and 94 pound pumpkin rated writing up in the “Echo” The Echo in April 1882 reports, “The residents in Newstead are becoming very much afraid that if a good supply of rain does not come shortly they will be destitute of water. The wells that in other seasons contained from 6 to 7 feet of water are now down to one and if the weather continues people will have to apply to Mr Marks who can supply from a well in his garden water for the millions. This gentleman has had erected a large windmill accompanied with tanks. It is the first that has been seen in Newstead, and will be of great advantage to his garden, as in a very short time a thousand or more trees can be well watered”. A levee bank was built to protect his place from the floods.

Another article reports on the purchase of a large and elaborate organ from the Melbourne Exhibition. “Apart from the fact that it is a complete orchestra in itself, it is a perfect master- piece of the cabinet maker’s art. Its height is over 11 ft, breadth 6 ft and depth 5 ft 6 in. It is comprised of ebonised wood, tastefully picked out with gold, and as a piece of furniture alone it would adorn a palace. But its chief charm of course, is the beautiful music it discourses. Every note of the music that one hears in a first class orchestra is represented in this wonderful instrument…….Mr Marks has been quite inundated with visitors since the instrument came into his possession, but he will be pleased to welcome all and sundry at ‘St Austell’ to give them an opportunity of inspecting and listening to it”.

Richard died in 1898 and Phillipa in 1900. They had no children After Phillipa’s death, the “Echo” reports on the auction that sold off 22 properties. Among them were 3 cottages in Campbell Street, 3 cottages and fruit trees in Panmure Street, a brick cottage on Cemetery Road, 2 cottages near the bank in Lyons Street, Dr Eccles’s house “Fintona” on the corner of Lyons Street and Wyndham Street, over 170 acres of grazing land as well his 4 roomed villa with 2 out bedrooms, a basement kitchen, a brick stable, coach house, blacksmith shop, large fruit shed, windmill and fruit trees. He had already gifted one property to his loyal tenant of 17 years, Mr Pryor the blacksmith.

The monument is of marble surmounted by a female figure with one hand resting on an anchor whilst the other is pointing upwards. It “reflects great credit on the workmanship of Mr W Redfearn of Castlemaine”.

(Information from copies of the Newstead Echo)

 

What happened to the villa and gardens? After the 1909 floods, it was decided to build the levee bank we have today and it is presumed that the site of the villa is under it.
