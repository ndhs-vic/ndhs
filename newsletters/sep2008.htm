ANNUAL PARTY

We hope our members are keeping in mind our annual party on Saturday October 11th at the courthouse from 6.30 pm. As usual bring a plate and drink to share. This is a night where there is no agenda but to enjoy the company of like-minded people.

NEXT EXCURSION

Spring is certainly with us and the nearby bush is in the midst of its brief flowering so it seems a good time to visit some of the gold mining sites in the Muckleford forest so that we can have the best of both worlds.

Some years ago we visited Frenchman’s Gully/Frenchman’s Reef. The gully was mentioned in mining reports from 1857 – 1860 and consists of alluvial workings. Today it is possible to find the remains of a puddler and several stone fireplaces. Then in 1889 two men found the cap of the reef at 40 feet and today it is still possible to see an adit into the hillside and shafts on the top of the hill. We intend to visit this area again.

Carpooling from the courthouse at 1.30 October 6th. Bring a mug and a plate to share.

 

LAST EXCURSION

Quite a group assembled to see the sites of the old hotels of the district last month. An article describing the Licence Reduction Board in 1913 appears in the October ECHO but for those of you who do not get a copy, it is reprinted here.

 

As mentioned in the August Echo, an excursion was taken to see the sites of hotels that existed in the 1913 era. The statutory population of the Newstead district was 2,305 and the number of hotels was eight, being two in excess of the number allowed by the Licensing Act. From the article in the Echo in July 1913, it is not altogether clear but it seems that the eight were the Newstead, Crown, Shamrock and Railway hotels in Newstead, the Farmer’s Arms at Sandon, the Captain’s Hotel at Welshmans Reef, another Shamrock hotel in Sandy Creek and at the junction on the Maryborough Road, the Loddon Leads.

Mr Burgess leased the Shamrock hotel in Newstead, closer to the town than the present Uniting Church and still in existence with its frontage directly onto the footpath. The eight-roomed establishment’s trade was decreasing and its closure was not contested.

The Farmer’s Arms Hotel still stands on the corner of Providence Gully Road, Sandon and Mr John Cameron was owner and licensee having purchased it from Patrick Bourke. The trade was chiefly bar trade but three bedrooms were available to the public and various witnesses attested that it was handy for people travelling to Newstead with stock.

A similar situation was the case of the Captain’s Hotel, Welshmans Reef. Ellis Griffiths was the owner and licensee. It had twelve rooms, also the post office and a telephone line to Newstead. It also had seventy acres of land so stock could be accommodated as well as travellers. Although mining in the district had declined it was hoped that there would soon be an upsurge in the near future. It was felt to have a better case for staying open than the Shamrock Hotel at Sandy Creek, which was closer to the competition from the Maldon hotels.

Then the case of the Loddon Leads was considered. (Today a windmill on the bend on the left-hand side of the Maryborough Road marks the spot) It was a two-story building of ten rooms in poor condition. Constable Jones who had been in charge of the Newstead station for four and a half years gave evidence that it had caused him a lot of trouble with after-hours trading. The licensee was Joseph Harte and he had several witnesses who were prepared to give reasons why it should continue. One farmer deposed that he would leave his young horse there when travelling to Newstead, as the noise of the trains would disturb it. Other nearby farmers also swore that it was well run and essential to them.

A week later the Licence Reduction Board gave its verdict. The Shamrock hotel Newstead and the Loddon Leads were delicensed, as also was the Shamrock hotel at Sandy Creek. Joseph Harte continued running his saddlery and the Loddon Leads was demolished. The bricks were used to build Kinross’s undertaking business – now the site of Helen and Trevor Wheeler’s pottery.

The Newstead Hotel was on the site of the playground opposite the Community Centre and burned down in about 1933.
