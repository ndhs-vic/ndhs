NOVEMBER – DECEMBER 2009 NEWSLETTER

 

MARKET

The gods smiled on us again – the change on Friday afternoon brought such strong wind that the tree adjacent to the courthouse land was uprooted and blocked off Wyndham Street and the rain that was predicted for Saturday held off until evening! Meanwhile the market was held with the minimum of fuss. Again we thank our members, the hard workers who turned up on the day and those of you from further away who contributed so generously to our Christmas Hamper raffle. It was the most successful raffle yet. The lucky winner was a local member, Joy Dodson who was greatly cheered at the sight of a car pulling up and an overflowing basket of goodies being delivered. The winner of the lucky stallholder number was Carol Quillerat who regularly supports our market. The amount raised was about $900.

 

P&ORA MEETING

On Thursday 19th November, the Society hosted the Pioneers and Old Residents association of Castlemaine. It was arranged that they would hold one of their monthly meetings at Newstead and afterwards see something of how our society works. It proved to be most successful. Our President, Derek Reid, welcomed the visitors and after their meeting gave a brief history of the society and its aims. The visitors were most impressed with the exhibition of Newstead Then and Now, the collection of photographs that Gail Leech put together for her exhibition in Dig café earlier this year. Also of interest was the display on the street names of Newstead that had been put together for the recent Family History Expo in Ballarat. The visitors were given a demonstration of the information available through our computer index and the newspaper microfilms before sharing supper with us. They expressed their appreciation and intend venturing this way again in the New Year when they intend to have a bus trip to Yandoit Creek and lunch at the Newstead Railway Hotel.

 

PHOTO OF MISS ANNIE BARKLA

We have recently been given a framed photo of Miss Annie Barkla. Miss Barkla lived close by the courthouse and was a respected member of the community remembered particularly for her catering skills. There is a connection with a recent photo we have been given of John Ritchie Wattie, the World War 1 soldier whose grave is beside the Glasgow War Hospital. A Glasgow resident who had got in touch with the Newstead Echo alerted us to this. Some research has uncovered that he had been engaged to Miss Barkla. So now, more than eighty years after his death in a foreign land, the two are have their images hanging side by side in Newstead.

 

The courthouse will be open as usual over the Christmas holiday period and the next meeting will be at 1.30 pm January 18th at the courthouse.

 

PADDY HANLEY’S GUNS

Recently this subject has come to our attention and included here is a précis of the writings of

S H Ebery F.R.Met S.

 Stan Ebery was a former resident of Sandon and from 1953 – 55, he wrote a series of articles for the Newstead Echo.

“Now in lieu of your eyes, keep your ears open and you’ll hear those peculiar and mysterious explosive sounds known locally under the title of “Hanley’s Guns”. It seems to me that the name Hanley has been immortalised through the medium of these sounds.

I wrote an article concerning them in 1913 which very soon afterwards struck the headlines in some of London’s daily newspapers. It has been rumoured that I gave them the title, but this is incorrect. They were so named long before my time – apparently by some Daylesford residents – though W H Ferguson Esq., geologist for many years to the Mines Department, was the first man to draw public attention to them in a short story he wrote in a magazine published by the Mines Department in the early part of the century. He first heard the name from a Yandoit resident.

In the Hanley family, when I first attended school at Werona, there were three ‘Pats’: grandfather Pat who got about with the aid of a stick, son Pat  and grandson Pat who was a schoolmate of ours and one of the best penmen turned out by the late Fred Andrew, our schoolmaster, who was a genius with the pen. The handwriting of some of his pupils was like copperplate. Of the hundreds of letters reaching me yearly, I have only to look at the writing on the envelope to assure me it’s from an Andrew’s scholar……

The late Mr Pat Hanley was a firm believer that the sounds when heard distinctly were a sure indication of rain. Just when a chap was in a spot of bother, cropping under sodden conditions, the old gentleman would come along and say ”Make hay while the sun shines boys, more rain is on the way; the guns are roaring again”.  His gloomy predictions were often correct, occasionally wrong, just as any other weather predictions are .To test his theory, I took observations of the sounds from 1911 t0 1940 when I left the district. These were placed against the weather conditions of the following three days. Pressures, temperatures, wet-bulb temperatures, humidities and wind directions, also rapid variations in anyone or all of these were recorded and correlated with the sounds…..I’m certainly not going to weary readers with the terrific mass of data collected over 30 years nevertheless there are two outstanding examples. The foundry whistle at Castlemaine was audible only under quiet, anticyclonic weather systems and their resultant fine weather. The 3 pm whistles from the Daylesford mines were heard only on the northeastern side of an anticyclonic cell…accompanied by unusually heavy frosts. …

It was originally thought in Daylesford that the noises were merely caused by people shooting rabbits on an estate owned by Mr Hanley, which lay about 8 miles to the north-east of the town but this was shown to be wrong when it was discovered that people living far to the north-west of Hanley’s property also heard the noises in a northwesterly direction. Neither was it due to blasting in the mines of Moorlort, for they did not cease when the mines were eventually closed.

I cannot say that subsequent observations made of Hanley’s Guns between 1914 and 1941 shed any further light on the probable origin of the sounds. A solution of the mystery appears to be as far off today as it was 100 years ago.”
